The scintillation light response and the decay time constant of Bi4Ge3O12 (BGO) were measured down to
a temperature of 6 K using a multi-photon counting technique. The light response of BGO was conﬁrmed
to be temperature independent below 100 K and an estimate of the light yield was made, giving a value
of 23,70072600 ph/MeV. The temperature dependence of the scintillation decay time constant is
interpreted using a simple three-level model for the emission centre which agrees well with the
experimental data over the temperature range investigated.

1. Introduction
Despite of rather low light yield at room temperature (not more
than 20% of NaI-Tl [1,2]) bismuth germanate (BGO) is regarded as a
very important scintillator material, due to its very good radiation
length (Xo ¼ 1.12 cm), reasonably short decay time (a fraction of a
microsecond), low afterglow, mechanical stability and an emission
spectrum matching well that of semiconductor photodetectors.
Soon after its characterisation as a scintillator in the early 1970s
[3,4] it became the material of choice of high resolution Positron
Emission Tomography (PET) [5]. In this application the light yield is
obviously a limiting factor and efforts were made to increase this
parameter.
BGO is an intrinsic scintillator and its emission is strongly
affected by a thermal quenching process; a decrease of temperature leads to an increase of luminescence intensity. Therefore,
cooling was identiﬁed as one of the ways to improve the
performance of the crystal as a scintillator. The change of BGO
scintillation properties with temperature has been studied by
Piltingsrud down to 77 K using a photomultiplier tube (PMT) and
it was shown that a threefold increase of the BGO light yield over
that at room temperature can be achieved [6]. This ﬁnding was
recently conﬁrmed [2] using an avalanche photodiode (APD) as
light detector. The lack of reliable photodetector that can operate
over a wide temperature range precluded studies of scintillation
properties below 77 K and no further low-temperature work has
been reported, except merely qualitative observations of BGO

scintillations at liquid helium temperature, using solid-state
photon counters [7].
The interest in low-temperature studies of scintillation materials has received a new impetus recently with the development of
hybrid cryogenic phonon-scintillation detectors (CPSD), operating
at millikelvin temperatures. These detectors combine a very low
energy threshold and an excellent resolution with the ability to
distinguish different types of particle interactions [8]. CPSD have
proven their advantage in experimental search for extremely rare
events, such as the interaction of Weakly Interacting Massive
Particle (WIMP) dark matter with nuclei, neutrinoless double-beta
decay or radioactive decay of long-lived nuclei (see Ref. [9]
and references therein). The power of the technique can be
appreciated by looking at the value for the half-life of natural 209Bi
(1.970.2  1019 yr) that has been measured recently by some of the
authors using CPSD with a BGO target [10].
The prospect of application of the CPSD technique in the multitarget cryogenic search for WIMP dark matter, EURECA1 [11], has
intensiﬁed research aiming at characterising scintillation properties of different materials down to cryogenic temperatures [9]. In
this respect, bismuth germanate is thought to be a very important
target, permitting a cross comparison with other detectors that
use germanium semiconductors such as cryogenic phonon
detector [12] or operating in conventional regime [13]. Due to
the above and due to a lack of knowledge of the low-temperature
scintillation properties, we initiated studies of scintillation light
response and decay time constant of BGO as a function of
temperature down to 6 K.

2. Experiment
The measurements of scintillation characteristics of BGO were
carried out using the multi-photon counting (MPC) technique.
A detailed description of the basic experimental set-up and the
data analysis procedure is given in the original paper [14] and
subsequent papers which present progressive improvement of the
technique [15,16].
For this study we used a 5  5  1 mm3 BGO crystal purchased
from Amcrys-H (Kharkiv, Ukraine). The crystal was mounted to
the holder of a conventional He-ﬂow cryostat (Oxford Instrument). An 241Am source (33 kBq) is located at 2 mm distance
from the sample. To reduce pile-up, we used a stainless steel
aperture with a hole of 0.1 mm diameter between the source and
the sample. The photon pulses produced by scintillation processes
were detected by a green-sensitive PMT (model 9124A, Electron
Tubes), recorded and analysed ofﬂine. The light yield is deduced
from a histogram of the scintillation pulse height and the decay
characteristics are obtained from the distribution of arrival times
of individual photons.

3. Results and discussion
Fig. 1 shows the temperature dependence of the scintillation
light response of BGO between 6 and 307 K for a-particle
excitation. As temperature decreases to 100 K the scintillation
light output increases steadily. At lower temperature, however,
the scintillation response remains virtually constant. The quenching temperature Tq (temperature, at which the light output
exhibits half of its maximal value) is found to be 250 K and the
total light output of the scintillator increases by a factor of 3.5
from room temperature (T ¼ 295 K) to 6 K. This behaviour is
consistent with what was reported in earlier studies of the
luminescence properties of the crystal using optical excitations
[3,17].
Provided that the ratio of scintillation signal amplitudes at a
given excitation energy of the a-particles and g-quanta (a/g ratio)
for BGO shows no noticeable temperature dependence, the
measured temperature dependence of the light response of BGO
at a-particle excitation can give a good estimate for the light yield
of the crystal in the low-temperature range.
Earlier studies by Sysoeva et al. [18] demonstrated that this
assumption is valid for BGO over the 253–350 K temperature

cryogenic experiments with a BGO target [10], carried out at
20 mK gave an a/g ratio equal to 0.21 at 5.4 MeV and 0.17 at
3.2 MeV. The ﬁrst value measured at the same energy (5.4 MeV)
agrees well with the data of Ref. [18] whereas the reduction of the
a/g ratio with lower a-particle energy is due to the energy
dependence of the a-particle’s ionisation density [19], being
proportional to the length of ionisation track [20]. The same a/g
ratio 0.20 was found for CaWO4 at room temperature [21] and at
few mK [22]. The results of studies of CdWO4 are also consistent
with this interpretation; the a/g ratio is reported to be 0.18 at
room temperature [19] and also at 12 mK [23]. Admittedly the
temperature independence of the a/g ratio is a rather common
feature of intrinsic oxide scintillators. Assuming therefore the a/g
ratio of BGO as temperature independent and taking the light
yield of BGO at room temperature to be 69007140 ph/MeV [2]
one can estimate the light yield of the crystal at 6 K as being
23,70072600 ph/MeV. Given that the light response of the BGO
does not change below 100 K, this number is very consistent with
a value of 22,70072000 ph/MeV at T ¼ 77 K, quoted by Moszynski
et al. [2]. The observed temperature dependence of the BGO light
yield is a common feature of intrinsic scintillators [9]; it manifests
a classic effect of the thermal quenching governed by thermally
activated non-radiative processes that compete with the radiative
ones.
Since the luminescence decay is controlled by the same
processes, the crystal exhibits a strong temperature dependence
of this characteristic. To the best of our knowledge, the lowtemperature scintillation decay constant of BGO has been
reported only for 195 [6] and 4 K [7]. The use of the MPC
technique allowed to carry out the measurements of this
characteristic over the entire temperature range of interest.
Fig. 2(a–c) shows the scintillation decay curves of BGO measured
at 295, 77 and 6 K under a excitation, respectively. The decay time
at 295 K was ﬁtted by a single exponential function and t was
found to be 0.4370.08 ms, while the room-temperature scintillation decay time constant of BGO is 0.3 ms [4–6]. It should be
mentioned that while being well suited for measurements of slow
scintillation processes and temperature dependences thereof,
the MPC technique becomes less precise when the main decay
time constant decreases to values below 1 ms [14]. This limitation
arises from the time resolution of the experimental set-up
currently in use.
Fig. 3 shows the variation of the decay time constants with the
temperature. At ﬁrst, the cooling of the sample from 300 to 150 K
causes a steep increase of the decay time constant. This is the
region where the kinetics of the radiative decay is strongly
affected by thermal quenching and the decay time curve is nearly
a single exponential function. Below 150 K the non-radiative
processes become less efﬁcient and therefore the augmentation of
the time constant is less pronounced. At this temperature, the
decay splits into two components and the best ﬁt of the
experimental data was obtained using a sum of two exponential
functions. The results of ﬁtting for 77 and 6 K are listed in Table 1.
The character of the t ¼ f(T) dependence changes again abruptly
below 50 K: the decay kinetics exhibits steep retardation until the
temperature reaches 10 K. The character of the temperature
dependence of the scintillation decay is very similar to that
measured by Moncorge et al. [17] and Rogemond et al. [24], using
ultraviolet excitation. It is also worthwhile remarking that the
measured decay time constant at T ¼ 200 K is 2.270.2 ms,
consistent with the number given by Piltingsrud [6] (2 ms at
195 K). Furthermore, the value of t ¼ 138 ms at T ¼ 6 K obtained by
us is very close to the estimate made by Cho et al. [7] (150 ms).

two models for the luminescence centre were proposed. The ﬁrst
model argues that the radiative transitions from the upper 3P1 to
the ground 1S0 state of the Bi3+ ion give rise to the main emission
band at 2.5 eV [3,25]. This model is also supported by calculations
of the BGO electronic structure, indicating that the bismuth states
are responsible for the dominant contribution to the top of
valence band and the bottom of the conduction band [26]. The
second model assigns the intrinsic luminescence of the crystal to
the emission of self-trapped excitons [17,24,27]. Finally, some
authors consider both, the mechanism of excitons recombination
and the emission of selectively excited Bi3+ ions [28]. Though very
complete and thorough, these investigations were carried out
using selective optical excitations, which is the principal difference with respect to the present studies. One should remember
that optical quanta used for luminescence excitation are absorbed
in a thin (a few nanometres) layer, while ionising a-particles
penetrate 10 mm below the crystal surface.
The character of the temperature dependence of the decay
time constant observed for BGO in the present study is typical for
the three-level model of the emitting centre constituting a ground
and two excited levels of which the lower one is metastable. Given
the electron conﬁguration of ground and excited states of Bi3+, it is
logical to assign the luminescence of BGO to this ion. In line with
this assumption the emission of Bi3+ at low temperatures
originates from the 3P0 metastable level, while at higher
temperature transitions occurs mainly between excited 3P1 and
ground 1S0 state. The respective model predicts the existence of
the low-temperature plateau in the t ¼ f(T) dependence and the
gradual decrease of the decay time constant with temperature,
caused by establishing a thermal equilibrium between two
emitting levels [29,30]. A further generalisation of the model
was achieved when the processes of the non-radiative quenching
of excited level to the ground state were taken into consideration
[15]. Eventually, the shortening of the decay time constant t with
increasing temperature T can be understood as the result of
thermally activated re-population of excited states and their nonradiative de-excitation:

The three-level model of the emission centre in BGO gives a
reasonable interpretation of the observed temperature dependence. Certainly, this is a very realistic and consistent model for
crystals with intrinsic emission at low temperatures when the
energy transfer processes are suppressed. A sensible approximation
in the intermediate temperature range is that the energy transfer
due to exciton migration can provide an additional channel for
radiative recombination. This channel is important when impurity
emission is considered [27] and the model might be improved by
including this feature. The derivation of the respective relations
should be the subject of the forthcoming studies.

4. Conclusion
Motivated by strong interest in the application of BGO in
cryogenic experimental search for rare events we measured for
the ﬁrst time the temperature dependence of the scintillation
decay time and the light response of BGO over 6–307 K
temperature range. It is demonstrated that the light yield of
the crystal increases steadily with cooling down to 100 K and
remains practically unchanged at lower temperatures. An estimate of the light yield of BGO at low temperature gives
23,70072600 ph/MeV, which is substantially higher than the
light yield of a CaWO4 scintillator (15,80071600 ph/MeV) [31].
The results of measurements of the temperature dependence of
the decay kinetics were analysed in the framework of the threelevel model of the Bi3+ emission centre. This model is well suited
for the interpretation of the low-temperature properties of this
intrinsic scintillator. Given that performance tests of BGO as
cryogenic phonon-scintillation detector prove to be very successful [10], we consider this material as a very attractive target for
cryogenic experimental rare event research.
